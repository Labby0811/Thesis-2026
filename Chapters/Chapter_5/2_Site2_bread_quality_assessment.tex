\subsection{Quantitative results}
The bread-quality branch produces image-level anomaly scores together with localization-oriented visual outputs. Since the anomalous reference set used in this work is small and externally sourced, the quantitative discussion is presented mainly in terms of anomaly-score trends rather than as a consolidated benchmark. Figures~\ref{fig:Anomaly_scores_good} and~\ref{fig:Anomaly_scores_defect3} report representative score plots for normal bread samples and for external defective samples, respectively.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{"images/RESULTS/Anomaly/good/scores_inference_graph.png"}
    \caption{Anomaly-score trend obtained on representative \emph{good} bread samples. The scores remain in the low-response regime expected for normal products.}
    \label{fig:Anomaly_scores_good}
\end{figure}

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{"images/RESULTS/Anomaly/Defect3/scores_inference_graph.png"}
    \caption{Anomaly-score trend obtained on external defective samples. Compared with the \emph{good} set, these clearly non-conforming loaves produce substantially higher anomaly responses.}
    \label{fig:Anomaly_scores_defect3}
\end{figure}

Although these defective examples are not fully representative of the industrial domain, the score separation provides a useful preliminary indication that the model reacts coherently to strong visual deviations such as burnt or mouldy surfaces.
