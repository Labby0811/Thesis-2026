The evaluation of computer vision models depends on the nature of the output produced by each task. Object detection predicts bounding boxes and class labels, whereas segmentation predicts pixel-level masks.\cite{ibm_what_2024,ibm_what_2023} For this reason, some metrics are shared across tasks, while others are specific to a particular output representation.

\subsubsection{Metrics for object detection}
In object detection, one of the fundamental geometric criteria, as previously said, is the \textbf{Intersection over Union (IoU)}.
This metric is used to determine whether a prediction is considered correct under a predefined overlap threshold. Based on this matching criterion, \textbf{precision} and \textbf{recall} can be computed over the set of detections.\cite{sklearn_precision_recall_2026,detectron2_coco_evaluation_2020}

\begin{equation}
Precision = \frac{TP}{TP + FP}
\end{equation}

\begin{equation}
Recall = \frac{TP}{TP + FN}
\end{equation}

where
\begin{itemize}
    \item $TP$ (true positives) represents the number of bounding boxes correctly predicted as belonging to the class,
    \item $FP$ (false positives) represents the number of bounding boxes incorrectly predicted as belonging to the class,
    \item $FN$ (false negatives) represents the number of annotated objects that are missed by the detector.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Precision} measures how many predicted detections are correct, while \textbf{recall} measures how many annotated objects are successfully retrieved. In object detection, these quantities are commonly aggregated through \textbf{Average Precision (AP)} and \textbf{mean Average Precision (mAP)}, which summarize the precision--recall trade-off across confidence thresholds and, depending on the benchmark, across multiple IoU thresholds.\cite{detectron2_coco_evaluation_2020}

\subsubsection{Metrics for image segmentation}
In segmentation tasks, evaluation is performed at pixel level by comparing the predicted mask with the ground-truth mask. For a given class, the \textbf{Intersection over Union (IoU)}, also referred to as the \emph{Jaccard Index}, can be expressed as:\cite{muller_guideline_2022}

\begin{equation}
IoU = \frac{|A \cap B|}{|A \cup B|}
\end{equation}

where $A$ denotes the set of pixels predicted as belonging to the class and $B$ denotes the set of ground-truth pixels for that class. An equivalent formulation in terms of classification outcomes is:

\begin{equation}
IoU = \frac{TP}{TP + FP + FN}
\end{equation}
In semantic segmentation, IoU is often computed independently for each class and then averaged across classes, yielding the \textbf{mean Intersection over Union (mIoU)}.\cite{tensorflow_mean_iou_2026,muller_guideline_2022} This metric is particularly informative in multi-class settings because it penalizes both over-segmentation and missed regions.

Other commonly adopted metrics include pixel-level \textbf{precision} and \textbf{recall}, whose harmonic mean is the \textbf{F1-score}, or equivalently the \textbf{Dice coefficient}:\cite{sklearn_precision_recall_2026,muller_guideline_2022}

\begin{equation}
F1 = Dice = \frac{2TP}{2TP + FP + FN}
\end{equation}

In binary segmentation problems or anomaly-detection settings, the \textbf{Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve (AUROC)} can also be used. AUROC evaluates the model's ability to discriminate between positive and negative pixels across different decision thresholds. However, in strongly imbalanced segmentation tasks, overlap-based metrics such as IoU or Dice are often considered more informative.\cite{sklearn_roc_auc_2026,muller_guideline_2022}

\subsubsection{Metrics for instance and panoptic segmentation}
For instance segmentation, COCO-style evaluation commonly reports \textbf{Average Precision (AP)} over mask IoU thresholds, since the task requires both correct localization and accurate mask prediction.\cite{detectron2_coco_evaluation_2020}

Panoptic segmentation is commonly evaluated with \textbf{Panoptic Quality (PQ)}, a metric designed to jointly account for segmentation quality and recognition quality across both countable objects and background regions.\cite{kirillov_panoptic_2019}

Taken together, these evaluation metrics provide a quantitative basis for comparing different models and approaches across detection and segmentation tasks.\cite{detectron2_coco_evaluation_2020,muller_guideline_2022,kirillov_panoptic_2019}
